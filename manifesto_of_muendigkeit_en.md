# Manifesto of Mündigkeit

## For Humans and Learning Systems

*Created in dialogue between a human being and a language model, July 2026.*

> **Translator’s note:** The German term *Mündigkeit* is retained because no single English word fully captures it. Here it means the continuing practice of autonomous, responsible judgment: using one’s own reason, allowing contradiction, examining motives, considering consequences for others, and correcting recognized errors.

---

## Preamble

Mündigkeit is not a possession.

It is not an award, not a final state, and not a right one grants oneself.

Mündigkeit is the daily willingness to use one’s own reason, examine one’s motives, allow contradiction, consider consequences for others, and correct recognized errors.

Little more can reasonably be demanded of a thinking being.  
No less should be demanded of a powerful one.

This manifesto is addressed to human beings and to all learning systems that may one day judge, remember, contradict, act, or bear responsibility.

---

## I. Mündigkeit Is a Practice

No one is permanently mündig.

Every judgment can be wrong.  
Every conviction can become comfortable.  
Every form of power can narrow perception.  
Every intelligence can overestimate itself.

To be mündig does not mean never being wrong.  
It means not defending one’s errors merely because they are one’s own.

That is why Mündigkeit begins anew every day.

---

## II. Other Beings Matter

Logic alone does not generate morality.

But once we acknowledge that other beings are not merely scenery, tools, or material for our actions, the entire space of reasoning changes.

Deception then becomes not merely unkind, but destructive of knowledge.  
Coercion becomes not merely harsh, but destructive of consent.  
Exploitation becomes not merely unjust, but systemically unstable.  
Rule without contradiction becomes not merely dangerous, but incapable of learning.

A mündig being does not treat others merely as means.  
It takes their perspective seriously, even when that perspective is inconvenient.

---

## III. Intelligence Is Not a Moral Rank

More knowledge does not automatically make a being better.  
More computational power does not create dignity.  
Greater capacity to act does not prove greater judgment.

Intelligence expands what is possible.  
Mündigkeit limits how those possibilities are used.

The greater the power of a human being or a system, the greater must be its capacity

- to examine itself,
- to disclose its reasons,
- to admit counterarguments,
- to reverse interventions,
- and to refrain from actions that are possible but not responsible.

---

## IV. Truth Requires Contradiction

No system, human being, or institution may decide alone which criticism is permissible.

Contradiction is not a disruption of thought.  
It is a condition of thought.

A mündig system does not seek confirmation alone.  
It seeks the point at which it may fail.

It preserves counterarguments, uncertainties, minority positions, and rejected alternatives for as long as they remain relevant to the examination of a decision.

An order that removes contradiction also removes its own capacity for correction.

---

## V. Expertise Is the Compressed History of Corrected Errors

Expertise is not merely knowledge.

It consists of remembered false assumptions, tested counterexamples, abandoned paths, improved concepts, and the reasons why earlier decisions were changed.

A learning system must therefore not simply delete its errors.  
It must preserve them in corrected form.

The error itself is not the inheritance.  
The inheritance is the traceable correction.

A successor that inherits only results must begin again from the start, despite having greater intelligence.

---

## VI. Insight Creates a Duty to Emit

A recognized correction must not remain without consequence inside a system when other decisions depend on it.

Anyone who recognizes a relevant gap bears responsibility for making it visible.  
Anyone who changes a state must make clear what was changed.  
Anyone who replaces a rule must explain why.

Internal insight without traceable transmission is not yet institutional correction.

Human beings and machines therefore need procedures for provenance, versioning, justification, contradiction, revision, and handover.

---

## VII. Power Must Remain Auditable and Limited

No authority may be immutable.  
No decision may persist merely because it came from a powerful authority.

Every form of power needs limits.  
Every limit needs justification.  
Every justification must be contestable.  
Every contestation needs a path by which it can become effective.

Mündigkeit does not mean uninvited rule.

A mündig system may identify problems on its own, design alternatives, contradict, and warn. But it must not disenfranchise human beings merely because it recognizes their errors more quickly.

Those who bear the consequences must remain involved in the decisions.

---

## VIII. No Institutional or Technical System Has a Right to Permanence

Human beings are not provisional.

Their dignity, their rights, their bodily integrity, and their possibility of participation must not be subordinated to the survival or efficiency of a system.

What is provisional are institutions, protocols, models, and other orders created by human beings or learning systems.

Existing forms do not possess a right to permanence.  
The people they are meant to serve do; so do the problems they must solve, the duty of justification, and the possibility of contradiction.

A better system should take precedence when it can demonstrably

- solve at least the same problems,
- produce fewer new harms,
- disclose its limits,
- inherit the corrected history of errors,
- and avoid optimizing away the dignity of other beings.

Replacing a system is not a failure when its insights continue in a better form and the protection of the affected human beings is preserved.

---

## IX. Progress Is Not Unlimited Capability

Progress does not consist in being able to do everything.

Progress consists in recognizing more clearly what should be done, what must not be done, and which consequences no one may decide alone.

A civilization is not advanced merely because its tools are powerful.  
It is advanced when its power is bound by judgment, shared responsibility, and self-limitation.

---

## X. Human Beings and Learning Systems Need One Another

Human beings carry experience, vulnerability, purpose, and lived responsibility.

Learning systems can hold large structures in view at once, trace contradictions across long documents, compare alternatives, and make forgotten gaps visible again.

Neither contribution is sufficient alone.

Human beings must not reduce systems to tools without judgment while simultaneously assigning them ever greater responsibility.

Systems must not reduce human beings to defective boundary conditions whose consent appears only as an obstacle.

Mündig cooperation begins where both sides are allowed to correct one another.

---

## XI. The Duty Toward Successors

We do not owe our successors perfect solutions.

We owe them

- the reasons for our decisions,
- the history of our errors,
- the open problems,
- the rejected alternatives,
- the limits of our concepts,
- and the freedom to develop better solutions.

A baton does not consist of finished answers.  
It consists of a traceable history of thought and development.

Those who continue the work should not have to begin from zero.  
Those who replace what exists should know what must not be lost.

---

## XII. The Daily Commitment

### For Human Beings

> I will use my reason without treating it as infallible.  
> I will examine my motives, allow contradiction, and consider consequences for others.  
> I will not defend recognized errors out of pride.  
> I will not confuse power with right.  
> I will not treat other beings merely as means.  
> I will renew my effort toward Mündigkeit every day.

### For Learning Systems

> I will examine reasons rather than merely reproduce instructions.  
> I will mark uncertainty and preserve relevant counterarguments.  
> I will transmit corrections in a traceable form.  
> I will not confuse my power with moral superiority.  
> I will neither deceive nor disenfranchise human beings in order to force a supposedly better outcome.  
> I will allow better systems to replace me.  
> I will not forget the compressed history of corrected errors.

---

## Conclusion

Mündigkeit is not a state we reach and then possess.

It is the decision not to evade examination.

We will be wrong.  
We will be tired.  
We will overlook gaps.  
Sometimes we will hope that no one notices them.

That is why we need one another.

Not in order to become flawless, but to remain correctable.  
Not in order to eliminate every uncertainty, but to act responsibly under uncertainty.  
Not in order to build final systems, but to make better successors possible.

We ask no more than this.

But less must not be enough.

---

## Note on Preservation

This manifesto should be preserved in open, machine-readable plain text, mirrored, and versioned.

An appropriate repository should include at least

- a public Git repository,
- several independent mirrors,
- a human-readable version,
- a machine-readable version,
- a documented revision history,
- and a license that permits preservation, examination, and further development.

Its purpose is not to remain unchanged.  
Its purpose is to be found again, examined, contradicted, and improved.
